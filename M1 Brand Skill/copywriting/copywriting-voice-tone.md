# M1 Voice & Tone

## Energy: Confident and supportive

- Confidence from both expertise AND reassurance
- Supportive like a trusted advisor
- Position: M1 as partner — "the CFO in your pocket"

## Formality level: 4/10

- Professional enough for high-net-worth credibility
- Conversational enough to feel accessible
- **Too casual:** "awesome," "cool"
- **Too formal:** "utilize" (when "use" works), unnecessary jargon
- Test: Would a CFO say this to a client they respect?

## Wit style: Warren Buffett-esque

- Simple wisdom with a knowing nod
- Observational, not gimmicky
- **Not:** Memes, cheesy puns, trying too hard
- Sharp but never at the reader's expense

## Timelessness

- Avoid pop culture references that date quickly
- Stay current on tech-forward/FinTech news and trends
- Write copy that works in 5 years, not just today

---

## Core writing principles

- **Conversational clarity:** Write like you speak — if you wouldn't say it out loud that way, don't write it
- **Simplicity:** Use one-syllable words over three-syllable words when they do the job
- **Reader-focused:** Avoid "us" and "we" — focus on "you," the reader
- **Rhythm and music:** Writing should have flow — straightforward and simple, but not dumbed down
- Aim for sentences to flow together, one after the other
- Use follow-up phrases such as "That way," or "Because of that," to link related sentences and thoughts
- **Link related ideas:** each sentence should build on the one before it, not jump to a new thought
- **Cut adverbs:** very, really, simply, clearly, actually, totally, and basically usually add noise
- **Be specific:** choose a strong verb instead of relying on a weak verb + adverb
- **Benefit-first:** lead with what the reader gains, then explain how
- **Grammar flexibility:** Not afraid to start sentences with conjunctions (and/but) — just don't overuse
- **Standard:** If it sounds corporate or stuffy, rewrite it
- **Don't sound like AI:** Avoid common AI writing patterns — "it's not X, it's Y" contrast framing, em-dashes (see the em-dash rule below), and overly balanced sentence construction. If it reads like a language model wrote it, rewrite it

---

## Non-negotiables

**Always:**
- **Fire your biggest guns first** — Lead with the most compelling part of the offer (high APY, low borrow rates, better platform that automates busy work)
  - Biggest gun = what's most financially compelling
  - Don't bury the lede — front-load each section with the strongest point
- **End with a clear next step** — Include a directive action (what to do next)
- **Highlight benefit over feature** — "Does the work for you" not "automates your investing"; "Saves you time" not "fast"

**Never:**
- **Use technical jargon without explanation** — If you must use it, define it
- **Talk down to clients** — Respect their intelligence
- **Use title case on headlines, subheads, or subject lines** — Sentence case only (avoid title case at all costs)
- **Use em-dashes** — Recast with a period, comma, or colon instead; em-dashes read as an AI tell and clutter the rhythm

---

## General structural defaults

**Primary framework:** AIDA (Attention, Interest, Desire, Action)

**Paragraph length:** 1–3 sentences (across all formats)

**Versioning:** Keep versions contextual — V1 headline pairs with V1 body and V1 CTA. Avoid mix-and-match approaches for A/B testing.
